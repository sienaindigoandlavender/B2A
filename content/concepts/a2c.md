## Summary

A2C describes the relationship between an AI agent and the human consumer it serves. The agent acts as a personal assistant: browsing, comparing, and recommending on the consumer's behalf. The human typically confirms before purchase in current deployments, but this is shifting toward greater agent autonomy.

## Why it matters

A2C is the demand side of B2A. For B2A to work, the consumer must be willing to delegate purchasing decisions to an agent. Understanding A2C — what consumers are comfortable delegating, what triggers trust, what causes refusal — is essential to predicting the rate and shape of B2A adoption.

## Current A2C implementations

- Voice assistants (Alexa, Google Assistant) for repeat purchases
- Copilot-style assistants for travel and software research
- ChatGPT with in-conversation checkout (September 2025)
- Gemini agentic checkout (piloting)
- Meta AI assistant transacting on WhatsApp/Instagram Shops

## Consumer comfort by category

| Category | Delegation comfort | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Commodity replenishment | High | Low stakes, high predictability |
| Travel (budget) | Medium | Criteria are clear; humans like to confirm |
| Travel (luxury) | Low | High stakes; emotional and aspirational |
| Software subscriptions | High | Rule-based; clear criteria |
| Fashion and lifestyle | Low | Taste is personal and hard to specify |
| Healthcare | Very low | Trust and liability concerns |

## Open questions

- How does trust in A2C agents develop over time? Is it earned through small delegations that succeed?
- Does the comfort level differ significantly by age cohort?
- What is the role of personalisation depth in A2C trust?
