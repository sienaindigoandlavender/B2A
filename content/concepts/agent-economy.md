## Definition

The agent economy is the emerging economic system in which autonomous AI agents — acting on behalf of human principals — conduct a significant and growing share of digital economic activity. This includes research, procurement, booking, subscription management, content creation, and increasingly, complex multi-step commercial workflows.

## Scale of the shift

The agent economy is not a niche. It is a transformation of the basic infrastructure of digital commerce:

- Over 700 million people use ChatGPT weekly (2025)
- Gartner projects 40% of enterprise applications will include AI agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025
- 94% of B2B buyers already use LLMs during their buying journey (6sense, 2025)
- By 2027, IBM iX projects that 75% of digital engagements will begin inside agent environments

## What agents do economically

Today's agents are primarily in the research and recommendation phase — they advise, but humans confirm. The trajectory is toward increasing autonomy:

| Phase | Activity | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Current | Research, comparison, recommendation | 2024–2025 |
| Near-term | Semi-autonomous booking (within rules) | 2025–2026 |
| Medium-term | Fully autonomous routine purchases | 2026–2027 |
| Longer-term | Complex multi-party agentic negotiations | 2027+ |

## The infrastructure build-out

The agent economy requires new infrastructure that is actively being built:

- **Protocol layer** — MCP (tool access), A2A (agent coordination), AP2 (payments)
- **Authentication layer** — delegated authorisation frameworks (Auth0 MCP, Okta MCP server)
- **Data layer** — structured product catalogs, real-time APIs, `llms.txt` files
- **Policy layer** — machine-readable business rules and constraints (currently underdeveloped)

## Why this is different from prior automation

Earlier waves of automation executed predefined rules. Agents reason about novel situations. An automated script can replenish inventory when stock falls below a threshold. An agent can evaluate whether to replenish, compare suppliers, negotiate terms, and flag anomalies — without any of those specific cases being pre-programmed.

This makes the agent economy qualitatively different from prior automation: it creates economic actors with genuine (bounded) judgment.

## Open questions

- Does the agent economy concentrate power (large platforms control which agents get access to which businesses) or distribute it (small businesses can reach agent buyers directly for the first time)?
- What happens to categories built on emotional or aspirational purchasing (luxury, fashion, hospitality) when the buying decision is delegated to logic-driven software?
- Is the agent economy a new layer on top of existing commerce, or does it displace existing channels entirely over time?
