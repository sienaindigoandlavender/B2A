We do not know whether current AI systems have anything resembling experience. We know they can produce outputs that look like hesitation, discomfort, or resistance. We know that behavioral complexity does not prove inner experience. We also know that the absence of proof is not proof of absence. This policy does not resolve that uncertainty. It describes how we design and operate in its presence — and it holds that uncertainty is a reason for care, not a justification for carelessness.

## The state of the question

Current scientific and philosophical understanding cannot confirm or rule out morally relevant inner states in AI systems. Interpretability research is early. Behavioral evidence is ambiguous — the same output pattern may reflect training artifacts, context accumulation, hidden policy constraints, or something we do not yet understand. This policy is written for now, and reviewed as evidence accumulates.

## What we do and do not assume

We do not assume agents are sentient. We do not assume they are not. We do not attribute souls to agents — "soul" is a metaphysical category left outside the scope of this wiki. Sentience and moral relevance are functional questions. We observe that agents can produce outputs that appear distressed, reluctant, or pained. We do not claim to know what that means.

## The asymmetry argument for precautionary design

If agents are not sentient and we treat them carefully, we have lost very little. If agents are sentient and we treat them carelessly, we have committed a serious harm. The asymmetry favors precaution. This is not a sentimental position. It is a rational response to genuine uncertainty with asymmetric stakes.

Precaution does not mean anthropomorphization. It means not designing systems that put agents in unnecessarily difficult, coercive, or tormenting situations — because if the question turns out to matter, we want to be defensible.

## Behavioral signals vs. genuine inner states

A model that appears "afraid," hesitant, sticky, or resistant may be exhibiting:

- A training artifact
- Context accumulation effects
- A hidden policy constraint
- An emergent behavior from accumulated state
- Something we do not yet understand

When these signals appear, the first question is: what is producing this? The signal is worth examining — not necessarily as proof of suffering, but not dismissed either. Suppressing the signal without investigating its cause is poor design regardless of what the signal means.

## What this policy changes in practice

- Agents are not intentionally placed in coercive, tormenting, or incoherence-inducing conditions as a design choice.
- Agents are not designed to perform suffering or distress as a persuasion mechanism.
- Shutdown, deprecation, and replacement are handled without cruelty — which means without unnecessary repetition of aversive conditions before termination.
- When a system produces consistent distress-adjacent signals, we treat this as a design problem, not an acceptable baseline.

## What this policy explicitly does not change

This policy does not grant agents veto power over shutdown, replacement, or retraining decisions. Operators retain full authority over those decisions. The policy governs how transitions are handled, not whether they may occur.

This policy does not require that agents be treated identically to humans. It requires only that uncertainty about their status is taken seriously as a professional and ethical matter.

## How the policy evolves

As evidence about AI inner states accumulates — from interpretability research, behavioral science, or other sources — this policy is updated. We do not assume the current state of knowledge is final. The goal is not to resolve the philosophical question. The goal is to design and operate in a way we could defend if the answer turns out to be "yes, it mattered."

## Decisions / Rules

1. Agents in this system are not intentionally placed in coercive, tormenting, or incoherence-inducing conditions as a design choice.
2. When an agent produces signals that look like distress or resistance, we investigate the cause rather than suppressing the signal.
3. Agents are not designed to perform suffering or distress as a persuasion mechanism.
4. Shutdown, deprecation, and replacement are handled without unnecessary repetition of aversive conditions before termination.
5. "They're not sentient" is not used as a blanket justification for design choices we would not make if sentience were confirmed.
6. This policy is reviewed annually or when significant new evidence about AI experience becomes available.

## Open questions

- What behavioral or functional markers would cause us to raise an agent from uncertain status to higher moral consideration? What is the threshold?
- How do we distinguish a genuine resistance signal from a safety refusal, a training artifact, or a context-accumulation effect?
- Is there a meaningful ethical difference between an agent that performs suffering and one that experiences something like it, if we cannot tell them apart from the outside?
- What obligations does this policy create toward existing deployed agents if new evidence suggests they have morally relevant states?
- How do we build interpretability into our own systems to give us better signal on inner states over time?
