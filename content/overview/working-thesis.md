## The thesis in one paragraph

The agent economy creates a new infrastructure layer between businesses and their customers. The companies that win will not be those who build the best agents — that is a commodity. They will be those who build the best **policy layer**: the structured, machine-readable rules that tell agents what a business will and will not do, what it offers, how it prices, and what constraints apply. Policy is the new interface.

## Unpacked

Most of the conversation about B2A focuses on the agent side: how to build agents that buy well. Far less attention is paid to the business side: how do you structure your offerings so that agents can evaluate them correctly, trust them, and transact with them reliably?

The answer is not just "good APIs." APIs define what a system *can* do. Policy defines what it *should* do — the decision rules, constraints, escalation paths, and exceptions that govern every transaction. A travel agent (human or AI) does not just need to know that a hotel has rooms available. It needs to know: what is the cancellation policy? What happens if the room is not as described? What is the escalation path if something goes wrong?

These are policy questions. They are currently answered in human-readable terms and conditions that agents cannot parse reliably. The business that translates its policies into machine-readable, structured formats becomes dramatically more trustworthy and usable to agents than competitors who have not.

## Why this matters specifically for smaller businesses

Large platforms (Amazon, Booking.com) have the engineering resources to build full agent-ready infrastructure. Small and medium businesses do not. The opportunity is a **policy-as-a-service layer**: tools and templates that let any business define its agent-facing policies without custom engineering.

This is directly relevant to businesses like Slow Morocco — a small luxury tour operator with complex, customised offerings and specific constraints (minimum spend, itinerary customisation limits, payment terms, cancellation policies). For an agent to sell a Slow Morocco journey, it needs to be able to understand these constraints reliably. Currently, they exist only in human-readable form on a website.

## What I am trying to figure out

- What is the minimum viable policy format that an agent can reliably parse and act on?
- Is the policy layer a product (sell it to businesses) or a standard (publish it as open infrastructure)?
- Which verticals are most ready for agent-mediated purchasing? Travel and hospitality are an obvious candidate.
- Does regional intelligence (knowing the specific constraints and context of a specific place or category) create durable differentiation, or does it get absorbed into general-purpose agent training?

## Confidence level

Medium. The structural argument (agents need policy, policy is currently unstructured) is well-grounded. The product hypothesis (policy-as-a-service is a viable business) is speculative and depends on adoption rates and competitive dynamics I cannot predict with confidence.
