## Thesis 1: The policy layer is the missing infrastructure

Everyone is building agents. No one is building the structured interface that allows businesses to be understood by agents. The gap is not on the agent side — capable agents exist. The gap is on the business side: most businesses are not legible to agents because their rules, offerings, and constraints exist only in human-readable form.

The company that builds the tooling for businesses to publish machine-readable policy layers will occupy a structurally important position in the agent economy.

**Confidence:** Medium. The structural argument is sound. The product hypothesis is unproven.

## Thesis 2: Regional intelligence is a durable moat in the agent economy

General-purpose agents have general-purpose knowledge. They know that Morocco is a travel destination, that riads exist, that the Sahara is impressive. They do not know which riads are actually good, which guides speak French and Amazigh, which routes avoid the worst tourist circuits, or what Christmas in the Drâa Valley is actually like.

This local, specialist, experiential knowledge — structured and machine-readable — is not easily replicated by training data. It requires presence, relationships, and maintained expertise. A business that can offer this knowledge in agent-accessible form has a genuine information edge.

**Confidence:** Medium-high. The knowledge asymmetry is real. The question is whether agents will expose this edge or paper over it with confident generalisation.

## Thesis 3: The window for first-mover advantage is 18–24 months

Agent consideration sets — the set of businesses an agent typically evaluates for a given category — are forming now. They will likely calcify over the next 18–24 months as usage patterns solidify and agent developers build in preferred data sources and tool connections. Businesses that establish agent-visible infrastructure now may hold durable positions similar to early SEO advantages.

**Confidence:** Low-medium. The analogy to SEO is suggestive but not definitive. The dynamics could be different — agent consideration sets might be more fluid, or platforms might maintain control in ways that prevent independent first-mover advantage.

## Thesis 4: Most AI businesses are not agent-native

The majority of businesses calling themselves "AI businesses" have added an LLM layer to an existing product. True agent-native businesses are designed from the ground up for agent-mediated commerce: API-first, policy-first, human-interface-second.

The businesses that will dominate B2A are mostly not in the "AI business" category yet. They are operators, platforms, and service businesses that understand their domain deeply and will add agent-native infrastructure to that domain expertise.

**Confidence:** Medium. This is partly definitional — what counts as "agent-native" — but the core observation about domain expertise mattering more than AI expertise feels right.

## Thesis 5: Luxury and experiential travel is an ideal early B2A vertical

The properties that make a vertical good for early B2A exploration: complex, high-value transactions where agent assistance is valuable; structured criteria that agents can evaluate (dates, guests, route, budget); but also specialist judgment where agent expertise is differentiated; and human principals who are time-poor and willing to delegate logistics.

Luxury travel matches all of these. The Slow Morocco use case is not incidental.

**Confidence:** Medium. Luxury travel also has properties that complicate B2A: high stakes, emotional and aspirational dimensions, and a traditionally relationship-driven sales model. How these interact with agent-mediated booking is genuinely unknown.
